# Pattern 1: Layer-by-layer traversal (spiral)

> Traversing a matrix in a spiral seems to require logic full of exceptions.
> The secret is not to think cell by cell, but in terms of **four bounds** that
> enclose the not-yet-visited part and **shrink** with each edge traversed.

<Challenge id="spiral-order" />

> Tried it? Without a structure, it turns into a tangle of `if`s and
> directions. The theory below organizes everything with four bound variables.

## The theory: four shrinking bounds

Picture four walls enclosing the unvisited region: `top`, `bottom`, `left`,
`right`. The spiral is always the **same sequence of four moves**, and after
each one you push the corresponding wall inward:

```text
1. top:     left → right    on row `top`        →  top++
2. right:   top → bottom    on column `right`   →  right--
3. bottom:  right → left    on row `bottom`     →  bottom--
4. left:    bottom → top    on column `left`    →  left++
repeat while top <= bottom and left <= right
```

The two extra checks (before the bottom and the left edges) cover non-square
matrices, where a "loop" can finish in the middle. Watch the bounds shrink and
the current cell follow the spiral — the light region is what is still left to
visit:

### Why is it O(m·n) and O(1) (extra) space?

Each cell is visited **exactly once** along the spiral — that's `m × n` cells,
so **O(m·n) time**. Apart from the output list (which is the answer, not
counted as extra), we use only four bound integers → **O(1) extra space**.
There is no way to be faster: you have to look at each cell at least once to
list it.

## The big idea: "layer by layer"

The spiral is a case of the **layer-by-layer** (or "shell by shell") strategy:
solve the outer ring, shrink to the inner ring, repeat. The same idea reappears
in **rotating the image** (next lesson, where you rotate ring by ring) and in
several matrix problems. When a grid problem "wraps the edges and funnels
inward", think of shrinking bounds.

> A common interview variation is the inverse: **generate** a matrix in a
> spiral (fill `1..n²` in spiral order) instead of reading. It is the same
> bound control — only writing instead of collecting. (It shows up in the final
> exam!)

## Summary

- Spiral = four bounds (top/bottom/left/right) that **shrink** after each edge.
- Always the same sequence of 4 moves; extra checks cover non-square matrices.
- **O(m·n)** time, **O(1)** extra space.
- "Layer by layer" reappears in rotating the image and generating a spiral.
