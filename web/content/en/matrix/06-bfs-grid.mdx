# Pattern 5: BFS on a grid (shortest path)

> DFS goes **deep** along a path before coming back. BFS does the opposite: it
> explores in **layers**, like concentric waves from the starting point. That
> order is exactly what answers "what is the **shortest** path?".

<Challenge id="shortest-path-binary-matrix" />

> Tried it? If you tried a DFS, you noticed it finds *a* path, not necessarily
> the **shortest** one. The theory below shows why breadth-first search (BFS)
> solves "the shortest" naturally.

## The theory: exploring in layers with a queue

BFS uses a **queue** (FIFO: first in is first out). You start at the initial
cell (distance 1), and at each step you take the oldest one out of the queue
and add its not-yet-visited neighbors (with distance + 1).

```text
queue = [(start, distance 1)];  mark start as visited
while the queue is not empty:
    (r, c, d) = take the oldest out of the queue
    if (r,c) is the destination → return d   (first to arrive = shortest!)
    for each free, unvisited neighbor:
        mark as visited
        add to the queue with distance d + 1
```

The magic: since the queue processes **all** the cells at distance `d` before
any at distance `d+1`, the search advances in layers. The **first** moment the
destination comes out of the queue, it arrived by the shortest path — there is
no shorter route, otherwise it would have been processed earlier.

### Why is it O(m·n) and why does it give the shortest path?

- **Time:** each cell enters the queue **at most once** (we mark visited when
  enqueuing, not when dequeuing — a detail that avoids duplicates). For each
  one, we look at a constant number of neighbors → **O(m·n)**. Space O(m·n)
  (queue + visited).
- **Shortest:** BFS expands by increasing distance. In an **unweighted** graph
  (each step "costs 1"), the first time the destination is reached is,
  necessarily, by the fewest steps.

> A detail that causes bugs: **mark the neighbor as visited at the moment you
> enqueue it**, not when it leaves the queue. Otherwise, the same cell can be
> added several times (by different neighbors) before being processed, inflating
> the queue and even breaking the count.

## DFS or BFS? The question decides

Both traverse everything that is connected and cost O(m·n). The choice depends
on the **question**:

| The question is... | Use | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| "does it exist? how many? cover the region" | **DFS** (simpler to write) | the order does not matter |
| "what is the **shortest** path? fewest steps?" | **BFS** | expands by increasing distance |
| "all paths / a specific path" | **backtracking** (DFS + undo) | needs to explore and come back |

## Summary

- **BFS** uses a **queue** and explores in **layers** of increasing distance.
- In an unweighted graph, the first arrival at the destination is the
  **shortest path**.
- Mark visited **when enqueuing**; O(m·n) time and space.
- DFS for "exists/cover", **BFS for "shortest"**, backtracking for "all paths".
