# Recognizing interval problems

> An interval is a pair `[start, end]` — a stretch of a line: a meeting time,
> a period, a piece of an axis. This is a short, breather section: almost
> everything in it starts with **a single move** that organizes the chaos.

> As always, every pattern lesson opens with a **🎯 Try it first**.

## The opening move: sorting

Intervals usually arrive **out of order**, and it's hard to reason about them
that way. The first reflex, in almost every problem in the section, is to
**sort** — by **start** or by **end**, depending on what you want:

- Sort by **start**: ideal for **merging** and **inserting** (you process the
  intervals in the order they appear on the line).
- Sort by **end**: ideal for **greedy** choices ("how many fit without
  overlapping?") — keeping the ones that finish early leaves more room for the
  next ones.

This step costs **O(n log n)** and usually dominates the solution's complexity.
After it, a single O(n) scan handles the rest.

## The key question: do two intervals overlap?

Almost the entire section revolves around **overlap**. Two intervals
`a = [a0, a1]` and `b = [b0, b1]` **overlap** when one starts before the other
ends:

```text
overlap  ⟺  a0 <= b1  AND  b0 <= a1
```

And the complement is useful: they do **not** overlap when one ends before the
other begins (`a1 < b0` or `b1 < a0`). Picture it on a line — two bars either
"touch" or they don't:

```text
[1────3]                     [1──────6]   ← overlap (they share [2,3])
     [2──────6]
                
[1──3]      [8──10]          do not overlap (3 < 8)
```

> A detail that always raises doubts: `[1,5]` and `[5,10]` **touch** at the
> point 5. Does that count as overlap? It depends on the problem statement —
> sometimes the end is "open" (a meeting that ends at 5 frees the room for
> another that starts at 5). Always check this convention in the examples.

## How to think about the section

The method is almost always the same: **sort** (by start or end) and do **one
scan** comparing each interval against the accumulated "state" (the last
interval in the result, or a count of overlaps). The creativity lies in
choosing **which endpoint to sort by** and **what to accumulate**.

## The section's problems

- Merge Intervals → *sort by start + merge*
- Insert Interval → *insert into a sorted set*
- Non-overlapping Intervals → *greedy by end*
- Meeting Rooms / Meeting Rooms II → *event sweep*

## Summary

- Interval = `[start, end]`; think of a **bar on a line**.
- The first move is almost always to **sort** (by start to merge/insert; by
  end for greedy choices).
- **Overlap:** `a0 <= b1 and b0 <= a1`; watch out for the convention on
  touching endpoints.
- General method: **sort + one scan** comparing against the accumulated state.
