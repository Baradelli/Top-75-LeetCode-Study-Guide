# Pattern 3: Greedy on overlap

> "What is the minimum number of intervals to remove so the rest don't
> overlap?" This kind of question is solved with a **greedy choice** — and the
> trick lies in sorting by the right endpoint: the **end**.

<Challenge id="erase-overlap-intervals" />

> Tried it? The temptation is to remove "whatever seems to get in the way the
> most." The theory below shows the simple greedy rule and why it gives the
> optimal result.

## The theory: keep the one that finishes early

Removing the **minimum** so nothing overlaps is the same as **keeping the
maximum** number of compatible (non-overlapping) intervals. And here is the
greedy choice, classic to the "activity scheduling" problem:

> Among competing intervals, **always keep the one that finishes earliest**.

Why? Because the one that finishes earliest **leaves more free space** to the
right for the next ones. Any other choice would occupy the line for longer
without gaining anything. So:

```text
sort by END
kept_end = -infinity
removals = 0
for each [start, e]:
    if start >= kept_end:    # fits without overlapping
        kept_end = e         # keep this one
    else:                    # overlaps the last one kept
        removals += 1        # remove this one
```

Sorting by **end** (and not by start!) is what makes the rule work: you always
consider first the one that frees the line earliest. Each interval that
**doesn't fit** after the last one kept is a removal.

### Why is the greedy optimal here? (the intuition)

Imagine there's an optimal solution that did **not** pick the interval with the
earliest end. You can swap its first choice for that one (which ends earlier or
at the same time) without making anything worse — it leaves at least as much
space as the other. Repeating the argument, the greedy "finishes earliest"
choice reaches the optimum. This "exchange argument" is the hallmark of correct
greedy algorithms.

### Complexity

The **sort by end** dominates: **O(n log n)**. The scan is O(n), with O(1)
state (just the last kept end and the counter). O(1) space beyond the sort.

## Where this greedy shows up

The same idea solves a family: **maximum number of meetings/activities that
fit** in a schedule, **minimum number of arrows to burst balloons** (each
balloon is an interval; arrows = intervals remaining after removing overlaps),
and variations of "select the maximum number of compatible intervals."
Whenever the question is "maximum that fits without conflict" or "minimum to
remove," think: **sort by end + greedy**.

## Summary

- "Minimum to remove" = "maximum to keep compatible" → **greedy**.
- Sort by **end** and keep the ones that fit (`start >= last end`); the rest
  are removals.
- Correctness via the **exchange argument** (the one that finishes early leaves
  more space).
- **O(n log n)** (sort), O(1) state; same idea in "maximum activities" and
  "arrows on balloons."
