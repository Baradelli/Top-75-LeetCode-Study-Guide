# Synthesis: which pattern to use?

> Three heap patterns covered. The section is short, but the reflex it trains is
> powerful: recognizing when the question is about **repeated extremes** and
> reaching for the heap.

## The section's decision tree

```text
Does the problem ask for the SMALLEST or LARGEST repeatedly, without needing everything sorted?
   → think HEAP. From there:

1. "The K largest / smallest / K-th / K most frequent"?
   → HEAP OF SIZE K  (K largest → min-heap; K smallest → max-heap)

2. "Merge / pick the next smallest among SEVERAL sorted sources"?
   → K-WAY MERGE  (min-heap with the head of each source)

3. "Median of a stream / balance two halves"?
   → TWO HEAPS  (max-heap of the smaller half + min-heap of the larger one)
```

And the general reflex: **heap = "what is the extreme right now?" in O(log n)**.
If you catch yourself wanting to re-sort at every step, or to compare repeatedly
to find the minimum/maximum of a changing set, that is a sign for a heap.

## The section's full map

| Pattern | Key question | Structure | Time |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Top-K | "the K largest/smallest" | heap of size K | O(n log k) |
| k-way merge | "next smallest among K sources" | min-heap of K heads | O(N log k) |
| Two heaps | "median of a stream" | max-heap + min-heap | O(log n)/insertion |

The idea that ties it all together: the heap keeps the **extreme accessible in
O(1)** and **readjusts in O(log n)** when the set changes. That is why it is the
"priority" tool — and it reappears as a **priority queue** in Dijkstra (graphs)
and in schedulers.
