# Pattern 1: Top-K with a heap of size K

> "The K largest", "the K-th smallest", "the K most frequent" — a whole family
> of problems. The key insight: you **do not need to sort everything**. All you
> need is a heap that keeps **only the K best** seen so far.

<Challenge id="kth-largest" />

> Tried it? Sorting the array and taking the right position works (O(n log n))
> — and it passes! But there is an approach that is better when K is small and
> the stream is large. The theory below shows the heap of size K.

## The theory: a fixed-size heap of K

To find the **K-th largest**, keep a **min-heap with at most K elements** — the
K largest seen so far. The trick is counterintuitive: to keep the **largest**
ones, use a **min-heap**, because that way the **smallest of the K large ones**
sits at the top, easy to discard:

```text
for each number x:
    push x onto the heap
    if the heap exceeded K elements:
        remove the top (the smallest) → the K largest remain
at the end: the top of the heap is the K-th largest
```

Why does it work? The heap always holds the K largest so far; its smallest
element (the top) is exactly the K-th largest. Any number smaller than that top
is discarded right away.

### Why O(n log k) — and when is it worth it?

There are `n` elements; each push/pop costs O(log **k**) (the heap never
exceeds K). Total: **O(n log k)**, with **O(k)** space. Compare it to sorting
everything (O(n log n), O(n) space): when **k is much smaller than n**, log k ≪
log n, and the heap wins — plus it works for **streams** (data that arrives bit
by bit), where sorting everything would not even be possible.

| Approach | Time | Space | Good when |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Sort everything | O(n log n) | O(n) | you need everything sorted |
| Heap of size K | O(n log k) | O(k) | K ≪ n, or a stream |

## Example: Top K Frequent Elements

*"The K elements that appear most."* It combines two patterns you already have:
**frequency counting** (hash map, from the Array/String section) + **top-K with
a heap**. Count the frequencies, then use a heap of size K over the pairs
`(frequency, element)`:

def top_k_frequent(nums, k):
    contagem = {}
    for x in nums:
        contagem[x] = contagem.get(x, 0) + 1
    h = []   # min-heap of (frequency, element), size <= k
    for elem, freq in contagem.items():
        heapq.heappush(h, (freq, elem))
        if len(h) > k:
            heapq.heappop(h)     # discards the least frequent of those kept
    return sorted([elem for freq, elem in h])

## The lesson: for the K largest, use a MIN-heap

The inversion is the confusing part — and that is why it is worth fixing in
your mind: **K largest → min-heap of size K** (the top is the "gatekeeper", the
smallest of the large ones, ready to be evicted). **K smallest → max-heap of
size K** (the top is the largest of the small ones). Keeping only K, and
discarding the wrong extreme, is what gives the O(n log k).

## Summary

- **Top-K**: keep a heap of size K with the K best; the top is the K-th.
- **K largest → min-heap**; **K smallest → max-heap** (the top is what gets
  discarded).
- **O(n log k)**, O(k) space — beats sorting when K ≪ n or in streams.
- Top K Frequent = hash map (count) + heap of size K.
