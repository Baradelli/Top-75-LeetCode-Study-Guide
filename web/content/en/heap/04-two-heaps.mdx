# Pattern 3: Two heaps (median of a stream)

> Finding the **median** seems to require the data sorted. But what if it
> arrives one at a time, in an endless stream, and you need the median **after
> every insertion**? Sorting everything every time would be very expensive. The
> elegant solution: **two heaps**.

<Challenge id="running-median" />

> Tried it? Re-sorting the list at every number is O(n log n) per insertion. The
> theory below keeps the median accessible in O(1), with O(log n) insertions.

## The theory: split into two balanced halves

The median is the "middle" of the sorted data. The clever idea: you do not need
*everything* sorted — you only need to know **where the middle splits**. So keep
the numbers in **two halves**:

- **`baixo`**: the **smaller** half, in a **max-heap** (its largest at the top
  — the largest of the small ones).
- **`alto`**: the **larger** half, in a **min-heap** (its smallest at the top
  — the smallest of the large ones).

If the two halves are **balanced in size**, the median sits right on the border
between them — at the tops of the two heaps:

```text
... 1  3  5 | 8  9 12 ...
   (max-heap baixo)  (min-heap alto)
        top=5         top=8

median = top of baixo            (if baixo has 1 more → odd)
       = (top baixo + top alto)/2   (if equal sizes → even)
```

For each new number, you insert it and **rebalance** the sizes (moving the top
of one heap to the other if needed), keeping the size difference at most 1. The
tops always give the median.

```text
on inserting x:
    push x onto `baixo` (max-heap)
    move the top of `baixo` to `alto`   (ensures baixo <= alto)
    if `alto` became larger than `baixo`:
        move the top of `alto` back      (rebalances the sizes)
median: top of baixo (odd) or the average of the two tops (even)
```

### Why is it O(log n) per insertion and O(1) on query?

Each insertion does a few push/pop on the heaps — **O(log n)** each. The median
is just **looking at the tops** — **O(1)**. Compared to "sorting every time"
(O(n log n) per insertion) or maintaining a sorted list with insertion (O(n)),
two heaps are unbeatable for a stream.

| Approach | Insert | Query the median |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Re-sort everything | O(n log n) | O(1) |
| Sorted list (insert in place) | O(n) | O(1) |
| **Two heaps** | **O(log n)** | **O(1)** |

## The lesson: balance between two structures

The "two heaps" pattern is a case of a more general idea: **keeping two extremes
balanced** so that the information you want (here, the median) is always
accessible at the border. The max-heap holds the top of the lower half; the
min-heap, the bottom of the upper half. Balancing the sizes keeps the median
"trapped" between the two tops.

> In Python, there is no max-heap: the trick is to store the **negated** values
> in `heapq` (so the smallest negated is the largest real one). In JS, just
> invert the comparator (`(a, b) => b - a`).

## Summary

- **Two heaps**: max-heap of the smaller half + min-heap of the larger half,
  balanced in size.
- The **median** sits at the tops: top of the max-heap (odd) or the average of
  the two tops (even).
- **Insert O(log n)**, **query O(1)** — ideal for streams.
- In Python, simulate a max-heap with negated values; in JS, invert the
  comparator.
