## Why array is the first section

Almost every LeetCode problem passes through an array at some point. The five
patterns you'll learn here — hash map, two pointers, sliding window, prefix and
Kadane — reappear in disguise in strings, matrices, intervals and even in trees.
Mastering this section is mastering the foundation.

## The general method (works for any problem)

Faced with any array statement, follow these 4 steps. They are the
"algorithm for choosing the algorithm":

### Step 1 — Solve it by hand first

Take the example from the statement and solve it **by hand**, without code. If
you can't solve it with pen and paper, no pattern will save you. This step also
reveals the special cases: empty array? a single element? negative numbers?
duplicates?

### Step 2 — Write the brute force (mentally)

What is the dumb solution that tries everything? It's almost always "for each
element, look at all the others" — two loops, O(n²). It's your starting point,
not your shame: in an interview, *stating* the brute force first shows you
understood the problem.

### Step 3 — Find the waste

This is the golden question: **what work does the brute force repeat
needlessly?** Each pattern in the section eliminates a specific kind of
waste:

| The brute force's waste | The pattern that eliminates it |
| --- | --- |
| "I search for the same value many times" | **Hash map** — remember what you've seen |
| "I compare pairs that obviously can't be the answer" | **Two pointers** — discard candidates in bulk |
| "I recompute the whole window that barely changed" | **Sliding window** — update only the edge |
| "I re-sum the same range many times" | **Prefix** — precompute running totals |
| "I re-evaluate every subarray start" | **Kadane** — decide locally: extend or restart |

### Step 4 — Check against the limits

The statement always tells you the maximum input size — and that **dictates**
the complexity you need (remember the Big-O table?):

| Limit on n | Required complexity | Translation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| n ≤ ~500 | O(n²) or even O(n³) passes | brute force may be enough |
| n ≤ ~100,000 | O(n log n) | there's probably a sort along the way |
| n ≤ ~1,000,000 | O(n) or O(n log n) | you need one of the patterns |

If your solution is O(n²) and n goes up to 10⁵, you already know the verdict
before submitting: Time Limit Exceeded. Go back to step 3.

## The method in action (no spoilers)

Take the classic problem: *"find two numbers that add up to a target"*
(Two Sum). Applying the method:

1. **By hand:** `[2, 7, 11, 15]`, target 9 → 2 + 7 = 9. Easy manually:
   you look at one number and *search* for the one that's missing.
2. **Brute force:** for each `nums[i]`, scan the rest looking for
   `target - nums[i]`. Two loops, O(n²).
3. **Waste:** on each pass I *search again, from scratch,* for a value in the
   array — even though I already went past it! If I **remembered** what I've
   seen, the search would be instant.
4. **Limits:** n up to 10⁴ — O(n²) barely passes, but the interview follow-up
   is certain: "can you do it in a single pass?"

"Remember what I've seen with instant lookup" has a name: **hash map** — the
next lesson. Every pattern lesson in this section follows this same trail:
first the waste, then the idea that eliminates it, then the problems where it
shines.

## The 8 problems of the section

These are the Top 75 problems we'll use as **examples** of the patterns (the
goal is the pattern, not the problem):

- Two Sum and Contains Duplicate → *hash map*
- Container With Most Water and 3Sum → *two pointers*
- Best Time to Buy and Sell Stock → *sliding window / running minimum*
- Product of Array Except Self → *prefix and suffix*
- Maximum Subarray and Maximum Product Subarray → *Kadane*

And on the final exam: three **brand-new** problems for you to apply all of

## Summary

- The method: **solve by hand → brute force → find the waste →
  check the limits.**
- The statement's limits **dictate** the required complexity.
- The 5 patterns of the section are 5 answers to 5 different wastes.
