# Pattern 2: Include or exclude

> A huge family of DPs boils down to a repeated decision: at each item,
> **take or skip?** The optimal answer is the better of the two choices — and
> that is the essence of decision DP.

<Challenge id="house-robber" />

> Tried it? The rule "you can't rob adjacent houses" creates the decision: if I
> rob house i, I can't have robbed i-1. The theory below turns this into a
> simple recurrence.

## The theory: the better of take and skip

In House Robber, when you reach house `i`, there are two options:

- **Skip** house `i`: the best stays whatever you had up to `i-1`.
- **Rob** house `i`: you gain `nums[i]`, but the best allowed before is the one
  at **`i-2`** (you can't take the adjacent `i-1`).

The answer for `i` is the **maximum** of these two choices:

```text
dp[i] = max(dp[i-1],            # skip
            nums[i] + dp[i-2])  # rob
```

It's the same 1D shape from the previous lesson, but now the recurrence expresses
a **decision** (max between include and exclude). And, since it only depends on
`i-1` and `i-2`, you can do it in **O(1) space** (two accumulators). Note the echo
of **Kadane** (Array): "extend or restart" was also a local decision — DP
has always been nearby.

## House Robber II: the houses in a circle

What if the houses form a **circle** (the first and the last are adjacent)?
The trick is to reduce it to the problem you already solved: either you **don't
rob the first house**, or **don't rob the last** — so run the linear House
Robber **twice** (on `nums[0..n-2]` and on `nums[1..n-1]`) and take the larger.
Reusing the linear solution as a building block is a powerful pattern.

## Greedy vs DP: the boundary (Jump Game)

Not every decision problem needs DP — sometimes a **greedy** suffices, and it's
cheaper. *Jump Game* ("can you reach the end, given that each position tells the
maximum jump?") has a natural DP, but a greedy O(n)/O(1) solves it:
walk through, tracking **the farthest reachable position**; if it never falls
behind the current position, you can make it.

<CodeRunner
  python={`def pode_pular(nums):
    mais_longe = 0
    for i in range(len(nums)):
        if i > mais_longe:
            return False                 # didn't reach this position
        mais_longe = max(mais_longe, i + nums[i])
    return True

print(pode_pular([2, 3, 1, 1, 4]))   # True
print(pode_pular([3, 2, 1, 0, 4]))   # False (stuck at index 3)`}

> When should you prefer greedy over DP? When a **locally optimal choice leads
> to the global one** (and you can prove it, like the exchange argument in the
> Intervals section). When you can't — when you need to **compare several choices
> and remember results** — it's DP. When in doubt, DP is the safety net: it
> always works, even if it sometimes does more work than a greedy.

## Summary

- Decision DP: at each item, **include or exclude** → `dp[i] = max(exclude,
  include)`.
- House Robber: `max(dp[i-1], nums[i] + dp[i-2])`, O(n)/O(1).
- House Robber II: run the linear one twice (without the 1st / without the last).
- **Greedy vs DP**: greedy when the local choice guarantees the optimum; DP when
  you need to compare and remember.
