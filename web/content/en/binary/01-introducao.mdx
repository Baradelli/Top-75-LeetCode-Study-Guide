# Recognizing binary search and bits

> This section has **two themes** that share the name "binary" but solve
> different things: **binary search** (finding something by cutting the search
> space in half) and **bit manipulation** (operating directly on the 0s and 1s
> of a number). We'll treat them as two modules.

> As in the previous sections, each pattern lesson opens with a **🎯 Try
> first**. Solve it before reading the theory.

## Module 1 — Binary search

The most powerful idea in the section, and maybe in the whole course: if the
data has an **order**, you don't need to look at each item one by one. Look at
the middle, and the order tells you which half to continue in — **discarding
the other one entirely**. Repeating this, the search space collapses:
n → n/2 → n/4 → ... → 1.

This "cutting in half" is what we call **O(log n)** (remember the Fundamentals
section?), and it is absurdly fast:

| n | Steps for linear search O(n) | Steps for binary search O(log n) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1,000 | 1,000 | ~10 |
| 1,000,000 | 1,000,000 | ~20 |
| 1,000,000,000 | 1 billion | ~30 |

Thirty steps for a billion elements. That's why recognizing "I can use binary
search here" is a game changer.

### The signs of binary search

- The array is **sorted** (or can be sorted without breaking the problem).
- The statement asks for something **better than O(n)** — almost always
  O(log n).
- You're looking for a value, a boundary ("first/last that..."), or even
  **the smallest value that satisfies a condition** (binary search on the
  answer — we'll see this on the exam!).

## Module 2 — Bit manipulation

Every number, internally, is a sequence of bits (0s and 1s). Manipulating bits
directly solves certain problems with an elegance and efficiency that no other
approach can match — and it's a skill interviewers love to test.

The tools: the operators `&` (AND), `|` (OR), `^` (XOR), `~` (NOT) and the
shifts `<<`, `>>`. With them you turn bits on, off, test them, and count them.

### The signs of bit manipulation

- The statement mentions **bits, binary, powers of 2**.
- "Find the number that appears once" / "the one that's missing" — the smell of
  **XOR**.
- A strange constraint: "without using the `+` operator", "O(1) space" in a
  counting problem.
- You need a **compact set** of flags (bitmask).

## The problems in this section

- **Binary search:** Search in Rotated Sorted Array, Find Minimum in Rotated
  Sorted Array → *classic and modified binary search*.
- **Bits:** Number of 1 Bits, Counting Bits, Reverse Bits, Missing Number, Sum
  of Two Integers → *fundamentals, XOR and techniques*.

And on the exam: three brand-new problems, including one that applies binary
search in a way that might surprise you.

## Summary

- **Binary search**: sorted data → cut in half → O(log n), fast enough for
  huge inputs.
- **Bits**: operate on the 0s and 1s with `& | ^ ~ << >>`; the signs are
  "binary", "appears once/missing" (XOR), space constraints.
- They are two independent modules within the same section.
