> on the bits, and introduces a trick worth gold: `n & (n-1)`.

## The theory: thinking in binary

An integer is stored as bits. `13` is `1101` in binary: `8 + 4 + 0 + 1`. Each
position is a power of 2. The **bitwise** operations work on all positions at
once:

| Operator | Name | Does | Example (4 bits) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `a & b` | AND | 1 only where **both** are 1 | `1100 & 1010 = 1000` |
| `a \| b` | OR | 1 where **either** is 1 | `1100 \| 1010 = 1110` |
| `a ^ b` | XOR | 1 where they **differ** | `1100 ^ 1010 = 0110` |
| `~a` | NOT | flips all the bits | `~1100 = ...0011` |
| `a << k` | left shift | pushes k places (× 2ᵏ) | `0011 << 1 = 0110` |
| `a >> k` | right shift | pulls k places (÷ 2ᵏ) | `0110 >> 1 = 0011` |

### The three tricks you'll use the most

From the operators, ready-made patterns are born:

| I want to... | I do | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **test** the last bit | `n & 1` | isolates bit 0; gives 1 if odd |
| **look at** bit i | `(n >> i) & 1` | brings bit i to position 0 |
| **turn on** bit i | `n \| (1 << i)` | OR with a one-bit mask |
| **clear** the rightmost 1 bit | `n & (n - 1)` | the star of this lesson 👇 |

## Example — Number of 1 Bits (with the `n & (n-1)` trick)

*"How many 1 bits are there in `n`?"* The direct solution: look bit by bit
(`n & 1`, then `n >>= 1`), 32 times — O(number of bits). It works. But there's
something smarter.

The trick: **`n & (n - 1)` clears exactly the rightmost 1 bit** of `n`. Why?
Subtracting 1 turns that bit into 0 and all the 0s to its right into 1; the
AND with `n` zeroes precisely that bit (and keeps the rest). So each
`n = n & (n-1)` removes **one** 1 bit — just count how many times until `n`
hits zero.

### Why is this efficient?

The bit-by-bit version always does 32 steps (the width of the integer). The
`n & (n-1)` version does exactly **as many steps as there are 1 bits** — if
`n` has only 3 bits set, that's 3 iterations, not 32. In both cases it's
O(integer width) = O(1) for fixed-size integers, but the trick usually does
much less work. O(1) space.

## Summary

- Numbers are bits; `& | ^ ~ << >>` operate on all positions at once.
- Essential tricks: `n & 1` (test the last bit), `(n>>i)&1` (look at bit i),
  `n | (1<<i)` (turn on bit i), `n & (n-1)` (clear the rightmost 1 bit).
- Counting 1 bits with `n & (n-1)` does only as many steps as there are set
  bits.
