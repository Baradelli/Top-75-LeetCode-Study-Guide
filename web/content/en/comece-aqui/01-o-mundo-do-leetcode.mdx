# What LeetCode is and why it matters

> If you've tried studying LeetCode before and felt lost — "why do I even
> need to know this?" — this lesson is for you. Before any technique, let's
> understand **the game** we're learning to play.

## What LeetCode is

LeetCode is a website with thousands of **programming problems**: a prompt
describes a task (e.g. "find two numbers that add up to X"), you write a
function that solves it, and the site runs your function against dozens of
automatic **test cases**. Green = passed all of them; red = failed one.

But the goal is never "solve all 3000 problems." It's something else.

## Why people study this

Two reasons, and both matter to you:

1. **Technical interviews.** Almost every tech company — from the giants to
   startups — uses problems in this style to interview. Being able to solve
   them is, in practice, what unlocks many job offers.
2. **Algorithmic reasoning.** Solving these problems trains you to see
   **structure** in a messy problem and to turn "slow brute force" into "an
   elegant, fast solution." That skill is valuable for programming as a whole,
   not just for the interview.

## Why you may have gotten stuck before

The most common mistake (and the reason so many people give up) is studying
like this:

> ❌ Read a problem's solution → "ah, got it" → next problem → when it's time
> to solve it on your own, complete blank.

This fails because you're **memorizing solutions**, not learning to think.
Each problem feels unique and new. It's like trying to learn to cook by
memorizing 3000 recipes, one by one.

The key shift: **the problems repeat in patterns.** Beneath prompts that look
completely different lives the same half dozen ideas. "Find two numbers that
add up to X", "find the substring with no repeats", "detect a cycle in a list"
— they seem like distinct topics, but each is an application of a **pattern**
you can learn once and reuse forever.

> Learning ~20 patterns gives you the tools to attack **thousands** of
> problems. It's the difference between memorizing recipes and learning to
> cook.

## What this course does differently

This course is organized **by pattern**, not by loose problems. And, more
importantly, it makes you **practice before teaching**:

- In each pattern lesson, you first get a problem to **try solving on your
  own** — you can get it wrong, you can get stuck, that's fine.
- Only then comes the theory. That's when it makes sense, because it answers a
  difficulty you **just felt firsthand**.

You'll hit the wall before you get the tool to break through it. That's how
learning sticks.

## What you need to know about programming

Not much. If you know the basics of **Python or JavaScript** — variables,
`if`, loops (`for`/`while`), functions, lists/arrays — you can follow along.
All the code runs right here in the browser, in both languages, and you can
edit and run everything. You don't need to install anything.

> Don't worry about "knowing everything" before you start. The course
> introduces each idea when it's needed.

## Summary

- LeetCode = problems + automatic test cases; it's used for interviews and for
  training your reasoning.
- Memorizing solutions doesn't work — **learning patterns** does.
- This course is pattern-based and makes you **try before you learn**, so the
  theory makes sense.
