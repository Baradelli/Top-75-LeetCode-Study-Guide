# Pattern 3: Two Pointers and expand from the center

> A palindrome is a word that reads the same backward: `racecar`, `level`.
> Two pointers **converging from the ends** verify a palindrome;
> two pointers **diverging from a center** find all of them.

<Challenge id="valid-palindrome" />

> Tried it? A common approach is to "clean" the string (strip punctuation,
> lowercase it) and compare it with its reverse — it works, but spends O(n)
> memory. The theory below does the same with two pointers and O(1) space.

## Theory 1: checking with converging pointers

The definition of a palindrome is symmetric: the 1st char must equal the last,
the 2nd the second-to-last, and so on up to the middle. That's literally the
two pointers from the Array section, on a string:

```text
left = 0; right = n - 1
while left < right:
    if s[left] != s[right]: not a palindrome
    left += 1; right -= 1
it's a palindrome
```

Why stop in the middle (`left < right`)? Because once they cross, all the
symmetric pairs have already been checked — continuing would just repeat the
comparisons. **O(n)** time (each char is visited once), **O(1)** space.

## Example 1 — Valid Palindrome

*"Is the string a palindrome, considering only letters/digits and ignoring
case?"* E.g.: `"A man, a plan, a canal: Panama"` → true.

The palindrome logic is the same; the twist is **skipping what doesn't count**.
Instead of cleaning the string up front (spending O(n) memory), the two
pointers simply jump over non-alphanumeric characters as they converge —
keeping space at O(1).

Even with the inner `while` loops skipping characters, each pointer traverses
the string **once** in total — **O(n)**, **O(1)** space (cleaning the string up
front would cost O(n) memory).

## Theory 2: finding palindromes by expanding from the center

Now the inverse problem: *how many / what's the longest* palindrome inside a
string. Brute force tests all substrings and verifies each one —
O(n²) substrings × O(n) verification = **O(n³)**.

The waste: each verification starts from scratch, ignoring that palindromes
have structure. The insight: **every palindrome has a center**, and it grows
symmetrically out from it. So, instead of testing substrings, pick each
possible center and **expand two pointers outward** while the sides match:

```text
for each center:
    left, right = center
    while within bounds and s[left] == s[right]:
        found a palindrome!
        left -= 1; right += 1
```

An essential detail: there are **two types of center**. Odd-length palindromes
have their center at a character (`aba`, center `b`); even-length ones have
their center **between two characters** (`abba`, center between the two `b`s).
That's why we expand twice from each position.

### Why is it O(n²)?

There are `n` center positions (actually ~2n, counting odd and even), and each
expansion costs at most O(n). Centers × expansion = **O(n²)** time, with
**O(1)** space. We went from brute force's O(n³) down to O(n²) by eliminating
the re-verification waste — and O(n²) is expected for "examining palindromes",
since the answer can have size O(n²).

## Example 3 — Longest Palindromic Substring

The exact same technique: expand from each center and, instead of counting,
**keep the longest palindrome** found (its start/end positions). Swapping
"count" for "record the maximum" is the only difference — again, the pattern is
the same, only what you harvest changes. O(n²) time, O(1) space.

> There's Manacher's algorithm, which solves it in O(n). It's clever and rarely
> required — expanding from the center (O(n²)) passes the vast majority of cases
> and is what you should master first.

## Summary

- Checking a palindrome: **converging two pointers**, O(n)/O(1); skip
  irrelevant chars with the pointers themselves to keep O(1).
- Finding palindromes: **expand from the center**, considering odd and even
  centers — O(n²)/O(1), much better than the naive O(n³).
- Counting or finding the longest use the same expansion; only what you harvest
  changes.
